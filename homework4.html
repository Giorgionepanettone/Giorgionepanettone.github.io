Statistical independence

Statistical independence is a concept in statistics that states that two events, AAA and BBB, are not influenced by each other. This means that the outcome of AAA doesnt impact the outcome of BBB, and viceversa. Formally, Pr[A=a∧B=b]=Pr[A=a]⋅Pr[B=b]Pr[A=a ∧ B=b] = Pr[A=a] \cdot Pr[B=b]Pr[A=a∧B=b]=Pr[A=a]⋅Pr[B=b].

For example, let’s say that A is a variable that represents the height of a population, and B represents the age. We expect the two to be indipendent because height has no relation to age. If we display the distribution of AAA and the distribution of A∣B=bA|B=bA∣B=b, for some b∈Bb ∈ Bb∈B, the two shapes will be very similar if not equal.

If instead we say that A represents the height and B represents the weight, we expect them to be dipendent. This is because intuitively, on average taller people weigh more.

Another formal way to express independence is through the formula Pr[A∣B]=Pr[A]Pr[A|B] = Pr[A]Pr[A∣B]=Pr[A], which is equivalent to the first formula presented at the beginning. It states tha the probability of A knowing B is the same probability of AAA, i.e. BBB doesnt influence AAA and thus knowing BBB does not change the probability of knowing AAA. This can become quite interesting when applied, for example, in the world of cryptography. One would ideally want that a plaintext AAA and its corresponding cyphertext BBB are independent, so that knowing the cyphertext reveals nothing about the plaintext. This exact concept is called Shannon perfect secrecy.
