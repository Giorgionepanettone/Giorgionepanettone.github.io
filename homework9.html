The Law of large numbers is a fundamental mathematical law that tells us something about the average of a sampled population with respect to its theoretical average. In detail, it says that the with the sampling size increasing distribution’s average converges to the theoretical one.
Formally, given a sequence of independent and identically distributed random variables (X1,X2,...,Xn)(X_1, X_2,..., X_n)(X1​,X2​,...,Xn​) with the same average Xnˉ=1n∑i=1nXi\bar{X_n} = \frac{1}{n} \sum\limits_{i=1}^n X_iXn​ˉ​=n1​i=1∑n​Xi​ and variance, given a theoretical average μ=E[Xi]μ = \mathbb{E}[X_i]μ=E[Xi​], we have that Pr(lim⁡n→∞Xnˉ=μ)=1 Pr(\lim_{n \to \infty} \bar{X_n} = μ) = 1Pr(n→∞lim​Xn​ˉ​=μ)=1
So if we want a more accurate result, we just need to increase the sample size of the population. This is easily visualizable by coding a program that just tosses a coin a lot of times and updates the resulting distribution with every toss. In the beginning we will have results that differ more or less slightly from the theoretical (50% heads, 50% tails) distribution, but as the program goes on the empirical distribution will converge thanks to the law of large numbers.
There are actually two versions of the law, a weak version and a strong version. Here we focused on the strong version.
