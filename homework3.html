Locations

A location in statistics is a value that represents the center, or more in general the tendency of a distribution. It’s easy to reason that given a distribution one could be interested in different tendencies, thereby assigning different weights (or importance) to the values of the distribution.
For example, one could be not interested in values too far from the center, and only want to capture values close to it.
Or one could be interested in knowing the asimmetry (a.k.a. skewness) of a distribution, applying a formula like the following:
1 n ∑ i = 1 n ( x i − μ ) 3 \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - μ)^3 n1​i=1∑n​(xi​−μ)3
This is called the Third Central Moment.
But why stop at the third? We can apply the Fourth Central Moment, which give us the kurtosis:
1 n ∑ i = 1 n ( x i − μ ) 4 \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - μ)^4 n1​i=1∑n​(xi​−μ)4
And this is just a type of locations. One could define a Geometric mean, which uses products instead of sums:
( ∏ i = 1 n x i ) 1 n \left( \prod_{i=1}^{n} x_i \right)^{\frac{1}{n}} (i=1∏n​xi​)n1​
Or a Harmonic mean, which is the reciprocal of the arithmeic mean of reciprocals, used when dealing with ratios:
n ∑ i = 1 n 1 x i \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{x_i}} ∑i=1n​xi​1​n​
By changing, for example, the exponent of the Geometric mean, we get different and new means (and locations).
It’s therefore trivial to see that there exist infinite locations, of many types!
