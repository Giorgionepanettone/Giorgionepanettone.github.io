Sampling mean and variance

We all know what happens when we sample a population and calculate variables like mean, variance, and all other kinds of statistic indicators we can think about.
But what if we calculate an indicator on these samples and study the indicators of this new set? There are interesting insights that come up.
The mean of the means is the same as calculating the mean of the parent distribution. The same goes for the mean of the variances.
The variance of the means is smaller than the parent variance. This is because the mean of a sample converges as opposed to the single values, thanks to the central limit theorem.

Lebesgue integral

The most famous and used integral is the Riemann integral. The main idea behind it is to slice the area that we wish to compute into many infinite vertical slices of infinitesimal values, and sum them all up. It just has one flaw. It only works with Riemann integrable functions (Mainly continuous functions). We can’t have functions with irregularities, or discrete functions.
This is where Lebesgue comes to rescues us. Instead of cutting the area into vertical slices, he had the idea of using horizontal slices, making non continuous functions eligible for integration. The Lebesgue integral is born.
It has a lot of applications in many fields, including Probability theory.
For example, it allows for density functions with irregularities, or to treat with a single formula both discrete and continuous casistics.
